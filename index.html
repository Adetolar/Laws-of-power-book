Inside the Book...

A quick glance at the Laws you'll learn

The law "Never outshine the master" suggests that one should avoid showing up or surpassing their superior in any way. In other words, it is better to keep a low profile and be subservient rather than to shine and overshadow one's boss or mentor.

The reasoning behind this law is that many people in positions of power are often insecure and easily threatened by their subordinates who appear to be more competent, skilled or talented. As a result, if someone outshines their master or boss, it may provoke feelings of jealousy or resentment, and potentially harm their position or prospects within the organization or relationship

The law "Never put too much trust in friends, learn how to use enemies" suggests that one should be cautious when it comes to trusting friends too much, as they may let you down or even betray you. Instead, it is recommended to keep friends at arm's length and be prepared to use enemies to one's advantage

The reasoning behind this law is that friendships are often based on emotions and personal connections, which can be unpredictable and subject to change. Even the most loyal friend may have conflicting interests or hidden motives that could conflict with one's own goals. In contrast, enemies are often more predictable and easier to understand, and can even provide useful information or leverage in certain situations

The law "Conceal your intentions" suggests that one should be cautious when revealing their true intentions, desires, or plans to others. Instead, it is recommended to keep one's intentions hidden, especially from those who might use the information to their advantage

The reasoning behind this law is that people are often motivated by self-interest, and may use the knowledge of someone's intentions to manipulate or undermine them. By concealing one's intentions, it is easier to maintain control over a situation and avoid being outmaneuvered by others.

The law "Always say less than necessary" suggests that one should be cautious about how much they speak and what they reveal to others. Instead, it is recommended to be economical with words and only say what is necessary to achieve one's goals

The reasoning behind this law is that people tend to reveal too much information when they speak, which can be used against them by others. Additionally, speaking too much can make a person appear insecure or unsure of themselves, which can diminish their influence and power in a given situation

The law "So much depends on reputation - guard it with your life" suggests that one's reputation is a critical aspect of their success and should be protected at all costs. One's reputation is the perception that others hold of them, and it can have a significant impact on their personal and professional relationships, opportunities, and overall well-being

The reasoning behind this law is that once someone's reputation is tarnished, it can be challenging to repair, and the consequences can be far-reaching. A negative reputation can lead to a loss of trust, credibility, and influence, which can ultimately harm one's chances of achieving success in various areas of life

ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Robert Greene

Robert Greene (born May 14, 1959) is an American author of books on strategy, power, and seduction. He has written six international bestsellers, including The 48 Laws of Power, The Art of Seduction, The 33 Strategies of War, The 50th Law (with rapper 50 Cent), Mastery, and The Laws of Human Nature.
